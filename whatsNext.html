What's Next?

The continuous search for exoplanets has opened doors not just to scientific revelations, but to philosophical, ethical, and sociological discussions. What does it mean if we are not alone? Philosophically, discovering an exoplanet harbouring life would pose pressing questions about our uniqueness. Is Earth merely a thread in a grand cosmic tapestry filled with a plethora of life forms? This is another concept discussed in “The Grand Design”. Hawking and Mlodinow discuss Richard Feynmann’s “sum over histories” and pose the idea that the universe exists in many different possible states (it took every path is could possibly take) and our conditions necessary for life on our Earth as we know might be vastly different to that of an Earth or another planet in a different reality (Hawking & Mlodinow, 2010). Religiously, many doctrines have beliefs around Earth and humanity; extra-terrestrial life might stir theological debates. From a sociological standpoint, confirming life elsewhere could shake the foundations of our societal norms. Would this knowledge create more unity between humans on Earth? Or would it create even more division? Scientifically and educationally, curriculums would be enriched with new knowledge and such a discovery could mandate huge shifts in our current scientific theories. It would be necessary to reassess life’s requirements. In terms of space exploration, habitable exoplanets could become focal points for further study, or perhaps even colonisation.

The Fishbowl Concept: Rethinking our Search for Exoplanetary Life

To truly fathom life’s potential, we must be prepared to challenge our traditional Earth-centric biases. Just as a fish might assume universal laws based on its bowls conditions, our terrestrial experiences can inadvertently narrow our definitions of life. While The Transit Method is a great start in search of life in other star systems, a leap of open-mindedness is essential to eventually understand how various ways life might manifest in the universe and allow us to broaden our search criteria to planets that aren’t just exoplanets. For instance, while Earth’s bio-chemistry is carbon-centered, life might centered around silicon or many other elements. Similarly, our preoccupation with the Goldilocks Zone and exoplanets could cause us to overlook life forms that flourish beyond it. For example, the subsurface oceans in moons like Europa or the atmospheres of gas giants could house life beyond our current imagination. We could broaden atmospheric targets by looking for alternative gas combinations that might provide clues about extra-terrestrial life forms. Focusing on the development of new technologies, particularly within the realm of computer science, it might be worth exploring areas beyond those currently observed using The Transit Method. Advanced computational models could potentially simulate environments with diverse core elements. Taking cues from Earth’s extremophiles underscores the importance of enhancing our current search strategies, promoting a comprehensive and interdisciplinary approach. By integrating insights from a broad spectrum of disciplines and staying open to the unfamiliar, we might uncover answers to our most profound questions about the nature of life and the cosmos.
