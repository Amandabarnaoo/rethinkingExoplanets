Life on Exoplanets?

Central to the quest for potential life in the universe is exoplanets. Several experiments and methods have evolved over the years in the field of astrobiology which have refined our ability to detect these distant worlds and analyze them for signs of life.The Goldilocks Zone refers to the orbital region around a star where conditions might be optimal for liquid water, a vital ingredient for life as we know it, on a planets’ surface (Brennan, 2023). Typically, the distance is between 0.95 and 1.37 astronomical units (AU) from the planets Sun. Although these boundaries may vary depending on factors such as the planets’ atmosphere, composition, and presence of greenhouse gases. In other star systems with different types of stars, the Goldilocks zone would be located at different distances from the star. Exoplanets residing in this zone, like our Earth, are of great interest to astrobiologists because of the potential they hold for hosting extra-terrestrial life.

Presently, the primary method employed in the search for exoplanets is The Transit Method. The Transit Method is one of the keystone techniques in exoplanet detection. As an exoplanet orbits and crosses in front of its host star from our point of view here on Earth, its causes a minuscule but detectable dimming in the star’s light (Brennan, 2023). The Kepler Space Telescope, which is famous for its exoplanet discoveries in various star systems, predominantly utilises the Transit Method. The Transit Method deeply intertwines with my academic study of Computer Science. Computer Science is all about data. The slight dimming of the star is captured by telescopes as data points over time, generating what we know as a light curve. Analysing these light curves is where computer science comes into play:

  • Data Processing:The sheer volume of data captured by telescopes like Kepler is immense. Efficient algorithms are essential to process and filter this data, distinguishing genuine planetary transits from other cosmic phenomena or instrumental noise.
  • Machine Learning and Pattern Recognition: Given the diverse range of stars observed automation becomes necessary. Machine learning algorithms are trained to recognize patterns of characteristic planetary transits, which enhance our detection capabilities.
  • Simulation: Computer simulations can model and predict the characteristics of exoplanets based on observed light curves, providing insights into their size, orbital period, and potential atmospheric components.

Current challenges with this method are false positives. Although scientists’ use algorithms to filter the data, sometimes the method can be misled by star spots or other celestial objects, leading to false detections. While the transit method is excellent at detecting planets, including those in or near the habitable zone, it does not provide direct information about a planet's habitability. It primarily helps astronomers discover and characterize exoplanets, including their size, orbital period, and sometimes even their atmosphere. To determine whether a planet in the habitable zone is truly habitable, additional information is needed, such as the planet's composition, atmosphere, and whether it possesses conditions conducive to life. Other methods, like spectroscopy and follow-up observations, are used to gather this information. So, while the transit method is a powerful tool for finding exoplanets, it doesn't directly reveal whether a planet is habitable. Drawing upon the fishbowl analogy, scientists often emphasize the significance of exoplanets within the Goldilocks Zone, largely influenced by our understanding of conditions we deem favourable for life. This focus might be a result of our limited perspective. It is possible that we are limiting our comprehension of life's potential by adhering too rigidly to the conditions we know sustain life on Earth and perhaps overlooking other planetary candidates. Life could plausibly exist in various regions of a star system: should we direct our telescopes towards areas we might not typically label as "habitable"? Relating this to my earlier assignment, extremophiles flourish in environments like scalding hydrothermal vents and acidic lakes. Similarly, life might exist on intensely hot planets outside the familiar confines of the Goldilocks Zone.
